Step 2: Put the Causes in Order

Now that you know what each cause was, think about when it happened. Drag the cards below into the order you think they occurred — from earliest to most recent. Some of these causes were ongoing conditions that stretched across years, so there's no single perfect answer. Do your best to sequence them, then submit to see how historians typically think about the timeline.

Why does order matter? In historical thinking, sequence is one of our most important clues about causation. Events that happened before a crisis are more likely to be prior vulnerabilities. Events that happened at the same time as the crisis are more likely to be triggers or early aftershocks. Events that deepened or prolonged the crisis afterward are extending vulnerabilities. The order you establish here will help you in Step 3.

    Step 3: Classify the Causes

    You've learned what each cause was and thought about when it happened. Now comes the historical thinking payoff: classifying each cause using the framework from your reading.

    Recall the three categories:

    • Prior Vulnerabilities — Existing conditions or events that occurred before the trigger. Weaknesses already present in the system.
    • Trigger Events — The event(s) that set off the cascade. The final rubber band.
    • Extending Vulnerabilities (Aftershocks) — Existing weaknesses made worse by the trigger, or new conditions created by it, that prolonged and deepened the crisis.

    Extending Vulnerabilities

    • Bank Runs
    • The Deflationary Spiral
    • The Dust Bowl
    A note on contested placements: Some of these could reasonably be placed in more than one category. Bank runs, for example, could be seen as a trigger (they caused banks to close) or an extending vulnerability (they were triggered by the crash). The Dust Bowl could be a prior vulnerability (overfarmed land was already depleted) and an extending vulnerability (the drought hit after the crash). Financial panic exists in both the trigger and aftershock categories depending on which moment you're looking at. This ambiguity isn't a problem — it's what makes historical causation interesting. Real events don't sort neatly into boxes.

    Nice work.

    You've done something historians do all the time: taken a complex event, broken it into its component causes, and thought carefully about how those causes relate to each other in time and in type. The Great Depression wasn't caused by one bad decision or one unlucky event. It was the result of decades of structural vulnerabilities, a triggering shock, and a cascade of aftershocks that no one fully anticipated.

    Keep this framework in mind as you move through the rest of the course and as you think about historical events. When you encounter Hoover's response to the Depression, or the New Deal programs that followed, ask yourself: is this a prior vulnerability, a trigger, or an aftershock?
