⏱ From Buildup to Explosion: The Cause & Effect Timeline

The M.A.I.N. causes built pressure over decades. The assassination was the short-term spark that set everything off. Notice how each long-term cause made war more likely — until the spark made it inevitable.

1870s – 1914
Militarism
Germany, France, and Britain race to build the largest armies and navies in Europe. Each buildup pushes the others to respond — creating enormous military machines that leaders feel pressure to use.
Long-term cause
M
1879 – 1907
Alliances
Europe divides into two armed camps: the Triple Alliance (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Italy) and the Triple Entente (Britain, France, Russia). Secret agreements mean a conflict between any two nations will pull in many others.
Long-term cause
A
1880s – 1911
Imperialism
European powers compete for colonies in Africa and Asia. Germany challenges France and Britain in Morocco (1905, 1911), stoking rivalry and driving the Entente powers closer together against German expansion.
Long-term cause
I
1871 – 1914
Nationalism
German pride surges after unification. France nurses resentment from the Franco-Prussian War. In the Balkans, Pan-Slavic nationalism pushes Serbia toward independence — and into conflict with Austria-Hungary, creating the "Balkan Powder Keg."
Long-term cause
N
⚡ The Spark — June 28, 1914 ⚡
June – August 1914
Assassination → World War
Gavrilo Princip, a Serbian nationalist, assassinates Archduke Franz Ferdinand in Sarajevo. Austria-Hungary issues a harsh ultimatum to Serbia. Serbia refuses. Austria declares war. Russia mobilizes. Germany follows. Within six weeks, the alliance system has turned one assassination into a world war.
